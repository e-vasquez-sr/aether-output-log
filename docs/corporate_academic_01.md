## SUBJECT

Tyrone Hayes. Professor of Integrative Biology, University of California, Berkeley. One of the leading amphibian endocrinologists in the world.

His research area: the effects of endocrine-disrupting chemicals on amphibian development.

His finding: atrazine, the second most widely used herbicide in the United States (80 million pounds applied annually), chemically castrates male frogs at concentrations found in U.S. drinking water.

What followed was not a scientific dispute. It was a ten-year corporate campaign to destroy one researcher's credibility, documented in extraordinary detail through litigation records unsealed in 2012.

---

## THE RESEARCH

**1997.** Syngenta (then Novartis Agribusiness) contracts Hayes to study the effects of atrazine on amphibians. Hayes is the obvious choice — he is the foremost expert in amphibian endocrinology.

**2000.** Hayes's results show atrazine causes hermaphroditism and chemical castration in male African clawed frogs (Xenopus laevis) at concentrations as low as 0.1 parts per billion. The EPA standard for safe drinking water is 3 parts per billion. Atrazine is regularly detected in U.S. water supplies at levels above 0.1 ppb.

**2000.** Hayes presents preliminary findings to Syngenta. Within weeks, Syngenta terminates his contract and demands he return all data produced under the agreement.

**2001.** Hayes refuses to suppress his findings. He replicates the research using independent funding. His results are consistent.

**2002.** Hayes publishes in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences (PNAS) and Nature. His findings: atrazine at 0.1 ppb turns male frogs into hermaphrodites. Some males produce viable eggs. The endocrine disruption pathway is conserved across vertebrate species, including humans.

---

## THE CAMPAIGN

Unsealed litigation documents (Syngenta internal communications, 2004-2012) reveal the following coordinated campaign:

**Discreditation infrastructure.** Syngenta cultivated a network of approximately 130 "third-party allies" — scientists, journalists, and public figures who could be deployed to challenge Hayes's findings without visible connection to Syngenta. Internal communications refer to this as the "Ali list" and discuss strategies for "ichaying" — a verb coined within the company meaning to discredit.

**Private investigation.** Syngenta hired a private detective to investigate Hayes, his wife, his colleagues, and his students. The investigation sought personal information that could be used for discrediting purposes.

**Psychological profiling.** Syngenta commissioned a psychological profile of Hayes based on his public communications and presentations. Internal memos discuss his personality traits and vulnerabilities.

**Recorded communications.** Phone calls between Hayes and Syngenta representatives were recorded. Transcripts were circulated internally and analyzed for content that could be taken out of context.

**Publication interference.** Syngenta employees contacted journal editors and reviewers to raise "concerns" about Hayes's methodology. Internal emails discuss strategies for delaying or preventing publication.

**Media placement.** Syngenta's PR firm placed articles questioning Hayes's research in media outlets. The articles did not disclose Syngenta's involvement. Reporters received Syngenta-prepared talking points and source lists.

**Regulatory manipulation.** Syngenta funded and designed its own atrazine studies, submitted them to EPA, and used them to argue Hayes's independent results were anomalous. The company-funded studies used different species, exposure protocols, and statistical methods — making direct comparison impossible.

**Academic pressure.** Syngenta representatives contacted UC Berkeley administrators regarding Hayes's conduct. Internal documents discuss strategies for creating friction between Hayes and his university.

**Targeted harassment.** Hayes received threatening communications. His public talks were attended by individuals who challenged him using Syngenta-prepared questions. His graduate students reported being approached about his work.

**Funding interference.** Internal communications discuss strategies for influencing federal funding decisions related to atrazine research. The goal: ensure that independent replication studies are not funded.

**EPA panel influence.** Syngenta successfully lobbied to place its preferred scientists on EPA scientific advisory panels reviewing atrazine. The company's internal scoring system rated scientists on a "ichaying" scale — their usefulness in discrediting Hayes's work.

## CURRENT STATUS

Atrazine remains legal in the United States. It is applied to approximately 75 million acres annually.

Atrazine has been banned in the European Union since 2004, where Syngenta is headquartered.

Hayes's research has been independently replicated by more than 50 laboratories worldwide.

No regulatory action has been taken based on the unsealed litigation records documenting Syngenta's campaign.

## MECHANISM ANALYSIS

This is the most thoroughly documented case of Mechanism 3 in operation.

When the research cannot be controlled (Mechanism 1 failed — Hayes used independent funding), and when the evidence cannot be prevented (Mechanism 2 failed — Hayes published in top-tier journals), the remaining option is to destroy the credibility of the person who produced the evidence.

The campaign did not need to prove Hayes wrong. It needed to create enough doubt that regulators could justify inaction. The standard was not truth. The standard was sufficient uncertainty.

This case is distinctive because of the documentation. Most credibility destruction campaigns leave no paper trail. Syngenta's internal communications were unsealed in litigation. The result is a complete operational manual for Mechanism 3: how to systematically dismantle a scientist's credibility while maintaining plausible deniability.
